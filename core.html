A True Story of Empire, Betrayal & Judicial Murder

He was a man who arrived at airports and parted queues. He built a biscuit empire across six nations and brought Coca-Cola back to India. He slept in Singapore's grandest colonial mansion and dined in the Dorchester. He never truly lost his temper, and he never fired anyone without regret.

On 8 July 1995, he died in Tihar Jail. His legs were blue and swollen. His bail had been denied three times. His Queen's Counsel had walked out of the trial in protest. His permanent address, as written on his death certificate by a municipal clerk, was Gate No. 4, Tihar Jail, New Delhi.

He had not been convicted by a single Indian court.

I
Chapter One — Origins

From Vendor's Corner
to the World

Thomas Aquino was sure of it the moment he looked at him: "Oh, you are so much like him. But I guess he is even taller than you." He was not. He was four inches shorter. But Rajmohan did not tell Aquino that. Height is not, after all, merely a physical attribute.

In the coastal town of Kollam, Kerala, a stamp-paper seller named Krishna Pillai became known simply as "Vendor." By the time he graduated from a horse-carriage to a Ford, no one called him a baron — but his grandson, Rajan Pillai, would earn that title across three continents. The cashew nut — brought to India by Portuguese colonizers from Brazil four centuries earlier — was the engine of this family's ascent.

Rajannan was sixteen years older than Rajmohan, closer to a father figure than a sibling. A stocky, dark man who arrived from Singapore and was mistaken for nobody special. Yet his name could part airport queues, summon locked immigration doors to open. When Rajmohan was stopped at Chennai airport without a tax clearance certificate, the mention of Rajannan's name — waiting at Changi — had him rushed to a plane seconds before the doors closed.

"What matters is not how tall you are, but how tall you stand."
— Rajan Pillai's own realization, told to Rajmohan

He was born almost exactly when India won independence — India on 15 August 1947, Rajannan four months later. The family astrologer made his horoscope and declared a new star was on the ascendant. Every subsequent astrologer endorsed the prediction vigorously. They were fortunate, Rajmohan writes: they did not live long enough to see his run of luck coming to an abrupt end.

"I was not a whit impressed when I first saw Rajannan. 'Is this stocky, dark man really my big, celebrated brother who lives in Singapore?' I wondered as I received him at the airport. I refused to accept that my brother who stood like a titan in all of Amma's stories could be this dark man who could easily merge in any suburban crowd in Kerala."
— Rajmohan Pillai, Chapter 1

Years later, when those pretty girls in Marco Polo, Singapore's glitzy discotheque, fell all over Rajmohan and hugged him — he realized then that they knew him only as Rajan's brother. Rajannan's shadow was long enough to shelter even those who stood in it unwillingly.

Chapter Two — The Empire

The Biscuit Baron
of Six Nations

When Rajannan was allotted land in Jurong, Singapore in 1973, he knew he was not going to remain a mere cashew trader. The factory at Jurong became so beautiful that workers called it their region's "Taj Mahal." Its hallmark product — Ole (Spanish for victory) — became the rallying cry of an empire.

1973 — Singapore Arrival
Rajannan lands in a city that was literally eliminating its crows — discipline made visible. He is given land in Jurong industrial estate for a factory. He sets himself two rules: the factory will be beautiful, and no one will reach the office before him. He arrives an hour before staff every morning. Anyone who beats him knows he is out of town.
🌰

Ole — Brand of Victory

Branded, roasted, elegantly packaged cashew nuts sold across Japan, Thailand, West Asia. The humble Kerala kernel transformed into a premium identity. Twentieth Century Foods' Jurong factory becomes a tourist attraction — workers call it their region's "Taj Mahal."

🍪

Britannia — The Crown Jewel

A 75-year-old Indian biscuit firm — passed from British (Huntley & Palmer) to American (Nabisco/RJR) hands — reclaimed and resurrected for India. At its platinum jubilee, he gives its seventh and last annual address: "From a small bakery in Dum Dum in 1918 to one of India's largest food companies — we have come a long way."

🥤

Coca-Cola's Return

After George Fernandes banned Coke in 1977, Rajan finds the re-entry. Through Britco Foods (60% his, 40% Coke), Coca-Cola re-enters India. A glittering Mumbai party celebrates — Gopikachechi ensures every detail dazzles. Then Coke's consultant finds a direct route, and they take it. Rajan flies to Atlanta to plead. He is turned away.

🏭

Six-Nation Biscuit Empire

After the $44M RJR Nabisco buyout in 1989, Britannia spans India, Pakistan, New Zealand, Malaysia, Hong Kong, Singapore. He defeats Arnott's of Australia for Griffin of New Zealand — Arnott's spokesman says: "Extremely disappointed." A bid that astonishes the region.

🌍

Thai Agri Foods — The Last Dream

A $103M Bangkok food company takeover — conceived while fighting criminal charges in Singapore. He paid 80–103 baht per share against a market price of 20 baht. Never-say-die. Shares crash the day he dies. Rajmohan spends two years putting it back on rails — for someone else's dream.

🏨

The Life of a Baron

Ridout Road mansion — Singapore's former British Resident's seat, 5 acres. Fort Aguada Hermitage. Holland Park, London (£11M p.a. household). Wimbledon marquee with politicians and players. Presidential suite, Shangri-La. Dorchester, London. His tips were often larger than the bill. He once hired a Learjet for Achan's 70th birthday.

"Singapore's Business Times headline: TRANSACTION MAKES LOCAL BUSINESSMAN BISCUIT KING OF INDIA AND PAKISTAN: US$44m deal with Nabisco. For years many people called him Biscuit King, on all kinds of occasions: when he rose, when he fell, when he was prosecuted, when he fled Singapore, when he was arrested in New Delhi and, on that most inappropriate occasion, when he died in Tihar Jail. That was no way for a king to die. Nor for a commoner."
— J. Rajmohan Pillai, Chapter 8

Janardanan Mohandas Rajan Pillai
1947 — 1995

The Biscuit Baron · Built empires. Never fired anyone.

The Man Behind the Legend

A Man Who Never Fired Anyone — And Never Let Anyone See Him Weep

Rajan Pillai never truly lost his temper. He never victimized those he disliked. He arrived before everyone else at his factory each morning — for years. His Chinese secretary Mei built Twentieth Century Foods on pure loyalty; she left only when Gopikachechi became his second wife. His operations manager Subha Napoleon deposited bail of S$4.5 million when every friend had made themselves scarce.

He could make the powerful feel at ease and the ordinary feel seen. Ross Johnson called him "a great entrepreneur, executive, person, and most of all — a friend." His senior counsel Alun Jones said the evidence showed he was "plainly innocent" — then walked off the case because the trial itself was "a perversion of a judicial process."

His liver was failing for years. He had two near-fatal haemorrhages — in Singapore and New Delhi. He kept it a secret. As a businessman, he did not want to unnerve his friends, clients and partners. When he finally disclosed it in Tihar Jail, the magistrate allegedly said: "I don't want to see your body parts."

Amma had convinced herself that there was in him a strong element of Karna, the tragic hero of Mahabharata, who could not help giving anything anyone ever sought from him. Rajannan was an uncompromising giver, arrogant and compulsive. His tips could often be bigger than the bill. He never expected reciprocation — not even gratitude.

"Mohan, just think of me, running from court to court like a haunted animal, hearing the howls and cheers of the onlookers of the media, seeking a sanctuary of justice — perhaps distant, perhaps non-existent. And look at Subha. Consider her courage, her sense of fulfilment . . ."

Rajan spoke these words to Rajmohan while resting in their family home in Kowdiar, Thiruvananthapuram — after he had escaped Singapore, after he had played with Rajmohan's children, and before he flew to New Delhi to surrender — and die.

III
Chapter Three — The Unravelling

Everyone Who Benefited
From Him Walked Away

The fall of Rajan Pillai was not one catastrophe but a cascade of betrayals — each by someone he had trusted, elevated, or enriched. Click each card to read the full account.

The Godfather Turned Enemy
Frederick Ross Johnson
CEO of RJR Nabisco · Failed $17B buyout · Filed 29-count fraud complaint
Johnson was the man who offered to be godfather to Rajan's son. Whose wife Laurie was made trustee of his will (1982). For seven years, genuine warmth. Then Johnson tried the biggest leveraged buyout in history — $17 billion for RJR Nabisco — and was outbid by KKR at $25 billion. He lost his job. When the partnership with Rajan later soured over repayment, Johnson filed 29 criminal counts with Singapore's Commercial Affairs Department. During the entire trial, Johnson and his key witnesses gave evidence — then never returned for cross-examination. Their untested assertions were accepted as fact. Rajan's six international business witnesses were rejected. Johnson's investment of over $30 million was virtually doubled in the settlement. Then, quietly, he retired to Atlanta alone.
Read full account
The French Takeover
BSN Groupe (now Danone)
Antoine Riboud · Entered as financial saviour · Seized six-nation empire
Antoine Riboud, the avuncular BSN chairman, walked Britannia's factory floors sniffing bread packets and declared "it would have been a tremendous mistake to go it alone — we don't know India." He seemed to mean it. But every financial rescue BSN offered came with strings. When it paid off a Hong Kong Bank loan that Rajan had planned to reschedule, the repayment became equity. The 50:50 balance tipped. BSN had brought in money; ownership follows money. Rajannan was naive, Rajmohan writes, to imagine that Riboud would never want control of a company in which he put in his money. When the time came, a 75-year-old Indian company — reclaimed from Americans by an Indian — passed to France. Rajan was out.
Read full account
The MD He Appointed
Sunil Alagh
Appointed by Rajan over two senior colleagues · Moved to BSN side
Alagh was Rajan's choice as Britannia's youngest-ever Managing Director, appointed over V.K. Singh and Balasubramaniam who had stronger claims. Rajan believed in marketing above hierarchy. As BSN's power became visible, Alagh moved toward the stronger sun. Rajan sacked him — rashly, by his own later admission. Alagh went to court, won permission to attend board meetings without voting rights, and had a ringside view of every operation even as the chairman's declared enemy. After Rajan's death, Alagh turned Britannia around and received the credit. Rajannan's genius for making friends, Rajmohan writes, was beginning to wane. He never knew how to deal with his enemies.

The Fixer from Bangkok
Rakesh Saxena & Chandraswami
Bangkok Bank of Commerce · "Done" with one syllable · S$50,000 dakshina
Rakesh Saxena was a Bangkok-based NRI who could move money through Bangkok Bank of Commerce with a single word: "Done." He was Chandraswami's ambassador plenipotentiary. Chandraswami visited Rajan in Singapore, promised acquittal, and received S$50,000 as dakshina. He brought Lee Kuan Yew's sister to Rajan's dinner table as evidence of his clout — barely six weeks before conviction. None of it worked. Saxena later offered Rajan "a passport to an obscure place in South America where no one would have reached him" — showing he knew Rajan faced prison or death. When Rajan died, Saxena reportedly said: "Poor man." After 1997's Asian financial meltdown, Saxena lost his mind. Chandraswami served a term in Tihar — the same jail where Rajan died.

Coca-Cola's Betrayal
The Fizz That Evaporated
Rajan opened India's door · Coke entered through Britco Foods · Then went in alone
Rajan unlocked India for Coca-Cola — a country that had banned the company since 1977 when George Fernandes drove them out. He created Britco Foods (60% his, 40% Coke) and secured Maharashtra land for the factory. A Mumbai party was thrown to celebrate — Gopikachechi ensured it was scintillating. But Coke's own consultant showed them they could enter India directly without Rajan's company. There was a clause in the agreement: if Coke found an independent route, it could take it. It did. Rajan flew to Atlanta to plead. He was turned away. The party was forgotten. The investment was not recoverable. Gopikachechi later filed a Rs 100 crore lawsuit against Coca-Cola.

Control of All Accounts
Nina Gopika Pillai — The Transfer
Sole signatory of all accounts · "No big money left" · Legal costs borne by Rajmohan
When the Singapore case began, Rajan transferred sole signatory rights of all accounts to his wife. P.C.D. Nambiar was aghast. Rajan cited Achan's example — Achan had always kept everything in Amma's name. When the anticipatory bail petition required the ability to furnish any amount the court might set, Gopikachechi said bluntly: "Rajan has left no big money with me for such expensive court cases." The legal costs — for all the futile bail applications, for the senior counsels — were borne entirely by Rajmohan, Rajan's younger brother. X
Chapter Ten — Enter Nina Gopika Pillai

Both Video Cameras
Failed

Both video cameras failed. That was strange. The cameras had been hired for a high-voltage function at Mumbai's Taj Mahal Hotel, which followed Rajannan's wedding with Gopikachechi. No one, Gopikachechi least of all, would forgive that failure — it meant there was no footage of the wedding. A suspicious mind might even have seen some design behind the incident.

Gopikachechi swept into Rajannan's life like an emotional storm and changed it completely. She was a naval officer's daughter, working as a front-desk manager at the Taj — the same hotel where Rajannan had a suite for months. Their meeting at the reception desk gave a new turn to Rajannan's life. Achan and Amma had to be cajoled to accept a daughter-in-law from a different background — Nusli Wadia assured them she was a good girl.

In those early years, Rajmohan found her disarmingly sweet. She was a guide and philosopher, teaching him how to hold his own among the rich and the famous. She engaged a coach for his tennis. She was jubilant when he beat her chosen opponent — as if she, not Rajmohan, was the winner. She taught him how important it was to be seen to compete for the best.

"She likes such things." — Rajan's silencing reply when a friend questioned his lavish gift-giving to Gopika

Rajannan could not bear her not getting anything she wanted. When she once walked out on him after a serious tiff, he went after her — and returned with something exorbitant inside a red ornate box. Burglars once broke into his London residence and made away with ornaments. Gopikachechi was not unduly disturbed. She merely replaced them.

"Rajan has left no big money with me for such expensive court cases."
— Gopikachechi, to lawyer K.K. Venugopal, as the bail application was being filed at the Mumbai High Court

After Rajan's conviction in Singapore, she held a party in their Mumbai villa, telling the assembled glitterati and media: "Rajan has escaped from Singapore." What should have been quiet legal maneuvering became public performance. That performance became evidence of arrogance. The front pages that followed used the word: fugitive.

When Rajan died, Bangkok civil court papers filed on her behalf listed Amma — Rajamony Pillai, very much alive, still praying her nightly prayers — as "dead." Amma filed a legal challenge. She was still praying for her son's soul when the papers were filed.

The Transfer of Control

When Rajan transferred sole signatory rights of all his accounts to Gopikachechi, his financial adviser P.C.D. Nambiar was aghast. Rajan merely said: "Achan always kept everything in Amma's name." Nambiar said nothing — the transfer was a fait accompli.

The crunch was to come later. When the anticipatory bail petition required the ability to furnish any amount the court might set, the money was simply not there. The man who had spent £11 million a year on his London household died with his younger brother paying the legal bills for his bail applications.

The Bangkok Court Papers

In Black Case No. 23456/1995 in South Bangkok Civil Court, Gopikachechi's attorney submitted on her behalf that "Mr Rajan Pillai's mother had died before him." The attachment listed Amma as "dead."

Amma, N. Rajamony Pillai — wife of Janardanan Pillai, mother of Rajan Pillai — has legally questioned the veracity of this report of her death.

She was very much alive. She was trusting her old gods. She was still saying her prayers.

XI
Chapter Eleven — The Strike of Saturn

Antoine Riboud
and the French Seduction

Antoine Riboud could have been mistaken for anyone other than who he really was: boss of BSN Groupe, one of the world's top five food firms. Slender in build and soft in speech, he moved about at an unhurried pace. When he walked Britannia's factory floors and sniffed bread packets, he seemed genuinely enchanted. That was a superficial veneer, as Rajannan discovered much to his dismay.

Riboud arrived in Chennai and immediately wanted to go to the factory. Once there, he walked around by himself, as if he knew the place well. He went to a street stall selling pav bhaji and bought a piece of Marathi bread just to taste it. France's grand old baker was making up his mind — and sealing Rajannan's fate.

The move put Rajannan financially at ease for a while, enabled him to be in command of the biscuit business in six countries. Eventually, it was that tie-up that broke him.

Rajannan was naive to imagine that Riboud would never want control of a company in which he put in his money. BSN's financial rescues were quietly becoming equity. When BSN struck a tough posture, presenting unalterable business terms, it had already made Rajannan increasingly dependent on its financial support.

"BSN Groupe had brought in money, and ownership and control always rested with those who brought in money. Sooner or later, Riboud's men would be calling the tune. Rajannan should have read the writing on the wall."
— J. Rajmohan Pillai, Chapter 11

BSN had already acquired from KKR some of RJR Nabisco's European subsidiaries. It paid off a Hong Kong Bank loan that Rajan had planned to reschedule — the repayment became equity. The 50:50 balance tipped. When the time came, BSN seized control of Britannia — bringing under French ownership a 75-year-old Indian company started by Britons, sold to Americans, reclaimed and run by an Indian for seven years.

"Business Today, December 1992: '...he seems to have run into an implacable foe in BSN, which is determined to move into Asia in a big way taking over the Britannia group—any which way it can. And unless he can buy off the French transnational soon, J.M. Rajan Pillai could just be ripe for the picking.' Yes. He was."
— Rajmohan Pillai, quoting Business Today
Chapter Twelve — God's Crooked Shadow

Rakesh Saxena & The Bangkok Miracle

Rakesh Saxena came from a family of bureaucrats. He had a postgraduate degree in literature. He started as a nondescript stockbroker in Kochi. No nameplates, no anteroom, no secretary. He sat at the far end of a hall that seemed too large for an office. He gave Rajmohan no visiting card. He spoke desultorily about the taste of wine and the perennial traffic jam in Bangkok.

Yet for the Thai Agri Foods deal, Saxena cut short their entire explanation of the $103 million request with a single word: "Done."

Chandraswami visited Rajan in Singapore while the trial was proceeding. He brought Lee Kuan Yew's sister as evidence of his clout. He promised acquittal. The dakshina — S$50,000 — was paid. A fortnight later, Saxena came to Delhi and asked for power of attorney over Rajan's Thai Agri shares. For once, Gopikachechi says, Rajannan answered with an instant and firm No.

Bangkok · Thai Agri Foods · His Final Dream

Saxena later offered Rajan "a passport to an obscure place in South America where no one would have reached him" — showing he knew, long before the verdict, that Rajan faced prison or death. When Rajan died, Saxena reportedly said: "Poor man." After the 1997 Asian financial meltdown, Saxena lost his mind. Chandraswami served a short stint in Tihar Jail — the same jail where Rajan died.

"Amma had an intuition that Rajannan was to suffer. All her prayers were: 'Give him, my Lord, strength to live it out.'

The Lord did not."
— J. Rajmohan Pillai, Chapter 11

XIII
Chapter Thirteen — Trial for Law, Test for Lawyer

The Perversion of a
Judicial Process

Ross Johnson filed 29 criminal counts. Singapore's Commercial Affairs Department pursued the case for years after Johnson and Rajan had already settled all disputes civilly — with all parties profiting from the settlement. In a normal legal jurisdiction, writes Alun Jones, Queen's Counsel: "that would have been that."

But Singapore pursued it. Jones flew from London to defend Rajan. Within three weeks, he had drawn a depressing conclusion: "The trial was a perversion of a judicial process." He discharged himself — a decision few lawyers would ever make — rather than be used as evidence of the trial's fairness.

Jones did not want to be the justification for his client's conviction. He returned his fees. He left a written statement of his reasons. He later wrote in The Spectator under the headline: "Who killed Rajan Pillai?"

R.V. Pandit, editor and publisher, had told Rajan before the verdict: "I am afraid Rajan might lose the case. I feel it in my bones. He should plead guilty if that would reduce his punishment." His apprehensions failed to have any impact on Rajannan, whose optimism was impervious to everyone's counsel.

"After all, he had six witnesses of international repute to support his account of events — straightforward, independent men from a variety of backgrounds — and every document which had turned up in pre-trial preparation had tended to rebut the allegation of cheating. Having re-read parts of the court transcripts, it is clear to me that no independent lawyer could have read the court record, which I possess in full, and concluded that Pillai had committed any serious fraud."
— Alun Jones QC, in The Spectator, after Rajan's death

The 29 Criminal Counts — What Jones Found

  • 2 technical offences of buying a company's shares with its own money
  • 22 counts in relation to the alleged sale of a cashew nut trading business at inflated value
  • 1 count of selling a trademark to the joint venture — which already owned it
  • 4 further counts — "total fraud said to exceed $13 million in value"

All disputes with Johnson were already settled. All parties had profited from the settlement. Ross Johnson's investment of over $30 million was virtually doubled. No widows or orphans had lost money. Yet Singapore pursued criminal prosecution. It cost Rajan $18 million to lose the case.

What Jones Observed in Court

  • Johnson's accountants called first to give "broad-ranging and shapeless assertions of guilt" before evidence was authenticated
  • Prosecuting lawyers openly "prompted and instructed in court by Johnson's own lawyers"
  • Witnesses "openly coached by lawyers outside the court"
  • Johnson and two key witnesses appeared once — then never returned for cross-examination
  • Their untested evidence accepted. Rajan's six international witnesses rejected.
"In any normal legal jurisdiction, that would have been that. No widows and orphans had lost money; all parties had gained; no obvious public interest lay in prosecuting someone for allegedly rising early enough in the morning to deceive a lethal corporate raider..."
— Alun Jones QC, The Spectator

XIV
Chapter Fourteen — Flight into Captivity

He Fled. They Came for Him Not as Policemen — But as Well-Wishers.

On 11 April 1995, a day before sentencing, Rajan Pillai walked through Changi Airport in Singapore on an almost-expired passport — using his father's initial, not his own name. Janardanan R. Pillai instead of Janardanan Rajan Pillai. Those who recognized him walked up only to wish him well.

M.P. Gangadharan, a Kerala politician who was in Singapore at the time, missed his dinner that night — Rajan had left too suddenly for the farewell. A direct fallout of Rajannan's departure: even the people closest to him were left without warning or ceremony.

In Mumbai, Gopikachechi threw a party. Champagne. Press conferences. Celebration. What should have been quiet legal maneuvering became performance — and the performance became evidence of arrogance. Fugitive was the word the front pages chose.

Hotel managers hid him in suites not on records. A senior politician provided a getaway car. A retired Director-General of Police drove with him through the night from Mumbai to Kochi. At Fort Aguada — the hotel he had helped build from nothing — he slept one final time in his own legend.

In Kerala, Magistrate C.D. Anil gave him bail. He played with Rajmohan's children. The Kerala High Court cancelled his bail. Every plea for anticipatory bail — Mumbai, Kochi, Delhi — was rejected. On 3 July 1995, CBI officers broke into Room 208 of Le Meridien, New Delhi, where Rajan was preparing to surrender the following morning. They did not give him that grace.

One dignitary even suggested a price for his help. It was a price the family could not pay instantly. Before they could think coherently about the deal, CBI officers descended on Rajannan.

"Rajan's arrest had become a matter of prestige for the police force. The decks are now clear for the CBI to arrest Pillai, if it can find him. 'If they can find him' — that was the operative clause."
Economic Times, April 1995

In Tihar, the man who had slept in presidential suites across the world is given a cement sleeping platform as a "special favour" — only because fellow prisoner George Kutty, a stranger booked at the US Embassy's complaint, pleads with others to release it for him. Jail doctor Venkatasubbiah, who had been sent an SOS an hour earlier, finally examines Rajan. He is transferred to DDU Hospital in a rickety ambulance. What Justice Leila Seth Found

Two years later, Justice Leila Seth's commission of inquiry confirmed: "There is no doubt that Mr Rajan Pillai was a sick man, having a chronic disease like alcoholic cirrhosis of the liver. He had two life-threatening episodes in 1992 and ten sclerotherapies till March 1995."

She found both jail doctors Venkatasubbiah and Hira Lal "casual and careless" — their negligence "eventually resulted in giving him hardly any chance of survival."

CBI counsel A.K. Dutt possessed the letter proving cirrhosis could not be treated in Tihar — and did not share it with the court. Leila Seth: "A responsible officer is expected to act in a manner showing a level of high integrity considering the fact that his silence might result in depriving a person of proper medical treatment."

XVI
Chapter Sixteen — A Search for Truth

The Elaborate Search
for What Was Already Obvious

The basic principle of effective non-governance, writes Rajmohan Pillai, is that nothing should be treated as self-evident. When the truth is obvious, governments can safely skip it by instituting an elaborate search for it. That was what happened after Rajannan's death.

That Rajannan was seriously ill was evident from medical certificates. That CBI counsel opposed even a medical check-up was public knowledge. That jail doctors did not do what they could to save a prisoner's life was obvious. Yet it was necessary to ascertain whether there was any negligence — and inevitably, it was to be a judicial inquiry.

Leila Seth, former Chief Justice of the Himachal Pradesh High Court, was appointed to hold it. At the end of her inquiry, she came to a startling conclusion. The conclusion, recorded as the first sentence of the last but one paragraph of the fourth chapter of her report, was:

Justice Leila Seth's Commission · Finding

"There is no doubt that Mr Rajan Pillai was a sick man, having a chronic disease like alcoholic cirrhosis of the liver. He had two life-threatening episodes in 1992 and ten sclerotherapies till March 1995."

Her report also chronicled the story of the missing letter — the court's urgent confidential inquiry to the jail's Resident Medical Officer, received by an assistant superintendent, sent without receipt, never delivered, never replied to. A letter that might have changed the course of events. A letter that simply disappeared.

"Unfortunately, both Dr Venkatasubbiah and Dr Hira Lal, the two doctors in Central Jail, Tihar, New Delhi, who examined him were casual and careless in the performance of their professional duty. Their negligence eventually resulted in giving him hardly any chance of survival."
— Justice Leila Seth, Commission of Inquiry Report

Yet where Magistrate Mehta was concerned, Leila Seth found she had no power to sit in judgement over a judge's orders. She gave a sixteen-page order to explain why. The man who had allegedly told Rajan "I don't want to see your body parts" was protected by judicial immunity. He received a promotion as additional sessions judge. He told The Economic Times he was "most unfortunate" about Rajan's death.

XVII
Chapter Seventeen — Gospel of Fear

A Theory of Conspiracy
in the Himalayan Dark

In the summer of 1996, about a year after Rajannan's death, Rajmohan and Gopikachechi set out to Dharmsala — to the monastery of the Dalai Lama. Gopikachechi had long wanted to meet two men: Nelson Mandela, and the Dalai Lama. Both were emblems of extraordinary endurance.

The road from Jammu to Dharmsala was less rough than feared. The lodging in Siddibara, some distance from the monastery, was more comfortable than expected. But Gopikachechi's mind was not on peace. She was fighting an election from Kollam, riding on a wave of sympathy. And she was still carrying her gospel of fear.

"Mohan, look at Mandela — he was given up for good. If anyone had not given him up, it was himself. Thirty years of despair did not break him."
— Gopikachechi, to Rajmohan, on the journey to Dharmsala

The meeting with the Dalai Lama had been arranged by Anand Sharma. His Holiness spoke softly, primarily about his struggle, for about twenty minutes. Rajmohan's mind was still troubled. The sinister scenario Gopikachechi had painted was weighing him down. The Dalai Lama's short discourse did not lift up his spirits.

In the terrifying silence of that Himalayan hotel that night, Gopikachechi revealed her theory of conspiracy — truth as she perceived it. She believed Rajannan had been coerced into signing a power of attorney in favour of Rakesh Saxena. She believed that the death was not merely negligent. That the pattern of things — hostile media, hostile officials, a spiritual godman who took his money and delivered nothing, expert medical care denied at the critical moment — could not have been a coincidence.

Leila Seth's Commission on the Conspiracy Theory

"She has made certain vague unconnected allegations regarding various persons being part of a conspiracy to kill her husband Mr Rajan Pillai but has not indicated the 'culprit brains'. The evidence of the applicant has to be tested on the anvil of cross-examination to ascertain whether these allegations are reckless, far-fetched, imaginary and false, fabricated or malicious."

The commission placed the burden of proof entirely on her. Every effort to get a judicial probe into her theory was being officially foiled until January 2001, six years after Rajannan's death, when the Supreme Court called for an inquiry.

The CBI's Double Role

It was the CBI which had opposed Rajannan's hospitalization, dubbing his plea for medical help as one not made in good faith. The CBI counsel had the letter that proved cirrhosis could not be treated at Tihar — and withheld it.

Ironically, the same agency is to investigate Gopikachechi's theory of conspiracy.

"One day, when we were going over things past, I told Amma: 'God had really conspired against Rajannan.' Amma was silent. I could not make out whether she endorsed my view or not."

Epilogue & Afterword

"What else could I do? I was merely trying to learn from him, watching how tall he stood."

— Rajmohan Pillai, younger brother, in the afterword

Two years after his death, Singapore's Straits Times reported Rajan Pillai had been "sighted in Darwin, Australia" — suggesting the body cremated in Kollam was borrowed, not his. The lie lived because the legend was too large to die quietly.

His mother Rajamony Amma was reported as "dead" in Bangkok court papers filed by his widow. Amma was very much alive — and filed a legal challenge. She was still saying her prayers for her son's soul, trusting her old gods, when the papers were filed.

No Indian court ever found him guilty of any wrongdoing. Every charge filed against him on Indian soil was thrown out. He died a man not convicted by any court that had heard both sides — only by one that stopped his Queen's Counsel mid-cross-examination and accepted untested assertions as gospel.

Achan had a stroke. He told Rajmohan: "I will not forgive Rajan for dying to give them an opportunity to drown us in an everlasting deluge of empty words."
